Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about Ontario take-home pay and how this calculator works.

Calculator Basics

What year are the rates from?
All rates used in this calculator are from the 2026 tax year, as published by the Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) and Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC).
How accurate is this calculator?
This tool produces a close estimate for most salaried Ontario employees with straightforward situations. It uses official 2026 rates and applies the basic personal amount. It will not match your exact payslip if you have RRSP deductions, other TD1 credits, employer pension plans, union dues, or taxable benefits.
Is my data stored or sent anywhere?
No. The calculator runs entirely in your browser. Nothing you enter is ever transmitted to a server. Your income and results disappear when you close or refresh the page.
Does this work for self-employed people?
No. This calculator is designed for employees. Self-employed individuals pay both the employee and employer share of CPP, may have different EI arrangements, and have different tax filing requirements.

Using the Calculator

Should I enter gross or net income?
Enter your gross annual salary — the amount before any deductions. The calculator applies all deductions to arrive at your net take-home pay.
Which pay frequency should I choose?
Choose the frequency that matches how your employer pays you: weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly, or monthly. This only affects how your annual net pay is divided — your total annual deductions stay the same regardless.
Can I compare job offers using this calculator?
Yes. Enter each gross salary separately and compare the take-home results. This is useful when two offers are stated in gross terms and you want to see what each actually means in pay.

Results and Paycheques

Does pay frequency change my annual deductions?
No. Your total annual deductions are the same regardless of pay frequency. Whether you are paid weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly, or monthly, you pay the same total amount over the year. What changes is only how that total is divided across pay periods.
Why might my actual paycheque differ from this estimate?
Several factors can make your actual paycheque different: RRSP deductions, employer pension plans, union dues, taxable benefits, additional TD1 credits, or mid-year changes to your income or status.
Why are my deductions higher than expected?
Five separate deductions come off your gross pay: federal tax, Ontario tax, CPP, EI, and the Ontario Health Premium. Each uses a different rate structure, and together they can total 25–35% or more of gross income for mid-range salaries. The calculator shows each deduction separately so you can see where the money goes.
Why is my take-home pay lower than my gross salary?
Your gross salary is reduced by federal income tax, Ontario provincial tax, CPP contributions, EI premiums, and the Ontario Health Premium. These are mandatory deductions for most Ontario employees. The calculator shows each line item so you can see exactly how much each one reduces your take-home.

Limitations

What deductions are not included?
RRSP contributions, employer pension plan deductions, union dues, taxable benefits, additional TD1 credits (spousal, disability, caregiver), and provincial tax reductions for low-income earners. These vary by individual and are not estimated by this calculator.
Does this apply outside Ontario?
No. This calculator uses Ontario provincial tax rates, the Ontario surtax, and the Ontario Health Premium. Other provinces have different tax brackets, surtaxes, and levies. If you work in another province, use a calculator specific to that province.
Why might CPP stop partway through the year?
CPP contributions stop once you reach the annual maximum for the year. Higher earners may notice CPP deductions disappearing from their paycheques partway through the year once they've hit the cap. This is normal — your paycheques will be slightly larger for the remainder of the calendar year.
